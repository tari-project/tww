 █      Revolutionizing Blockchain Education: Tari Labs University 2.0      ▓
  ▀▓▀░▒▀■█▀█▒▀▄▀▓■▓▒ ░▒▀■█▀█▒▀▄▀▓■▓░▒▀■█▀█▒▀▄▀▓■▓▒░▒▀■▓▒░▓▒░▒▀■█▀█▒▀▄▀▓■▓▒▀█

Tari Labs University (TLU), initially launched in 2019, has undergone a
significant transformation with the introduction of TLU 2.0. The original
platform, designed to educate developers about blockchain technology through
seminars and published reports, has now expanded its scope and content. The new
version retains its foundational material but adds a wealth of curated content
aimed at bridging the gap between a curious developer and a proficient
blockchain engineer. What makes TLU 2.0 stand out is its comprehensive approach
to learning. It features a range of courses, such as the "Introduction to
Blockchain," which starts from the basics and progresses to advanced topics,
including an in-depth analysis of the Bitcoin white paper. The platform also
introduces interactive tutorials using the latest Tari cryptography libraries,
allowing users to engage with the material in a hands-on manner.

The update is not just about content; it's also about accessibility and
community involvement. TLU 2.0 has implemented category tags for easy navigation
and a local search index, enhanced by TariBot, to assist users in finding
relevant information quickly. The platform encourages community contributions,
inviting users to submit new content, improve existing materials, or even
suggest self-assessment questions. Looking ahead, TLU plans to expand its
offerings to include topics like zero-knowledge proving systems and smart
contracts, in anticipation of Tari's mainnet launch. This evolution of TLU
signifies a commitment to not just educating but also building a community of
knowledgeable developers in the blockchain space.

https://www.tari.com/updates/2024-01-17-update-126

 █            Shattering Bitcoin's Illusion of Anonymity in 2013            ▓
  ▀▓▀░▒▀■█▀█▒▀▄▀▓■▓▒ ░▒▀■█▀█▒▀▄▀▓■▓░▒▀■█▀█▒▀▄▀▓■▓▒░▒▀■▓▒░▓▒░▒▀■█▀█▒▀▄▀▓■▓▒▀█

The article "How a 27-year-old busted the myth of Bitcoin’s anonymity" delves
into the groundbreaking work of Sarah Meiklejohn, a young mathematician who
challenged the widely held belief that Bitcoin transactions are untraceable.
Initially perceived as a digital haven for privacy, Bitcoin's supposed anonymity
was a major draw for users ranging from crypto-anarchists to individuals engaged
in illicit activities. This belief was bolstered by the cryptocurrency's use on
platforms like the Silk Road, a dark-web market for illegal drugs and other
contraband. However, Meiklejohn's research in 2013 revealed a starkly different
reality. Through her extensive and varied use of Bitcoin - involving hundreds of
transactions, including purchases, bets, and transfers - she meticulously
documented and analyzed the blockchain's data. Her goal was to test Bitcoin's
privacy features in a way never done before.

Meiklejohn's experiments were not just academic exercises but a deep dive into
the Bitcoin ecosystem, exploring how people used the cryptocurrency and whether
they were spending or saving it. Her findings were revolutionary, demonstrating
that Bitcoin transactions could often be traced, contrary to the popular belief
of anonymity. This revelation had profound implications, leading to significant
advancements in crypto criminal justice. It enabled the solving of major
cybercrimes, including the bust of the largest child sexual abuse video site on
the dark web and the seizure of stolen bitcoins worth hundreds of millions of
dollars. Meiklejohn's work marked a turning point in understanding
cryptocurrency's privacy properties and sparked a new era in the fight against
cybercrime, challenging the notion of Bitcoin as the ultimate privacy-preserving
currency.

https://arstechnica.com/features/2024/01/how-a-27-year-old-busted-the-myth-of-
bitcoins-anonymity/

 █           Google Street View's German Gap: Privacy Over Pixels           ▓
  ▀▓▀░▒▀■█▀█▒▀▄▀▓■▓▒ ░▒▀■█▀█▒▀▄▀▓■▓░▒▀■█▀█▒▀▄▀▓■▓▒░▒▀■▓▒░▓▒░▒▀■█▀█▒▀▄▀▓■▓▒▀█

The article "Why Germany is a blank spot on Google’s Street View" explores the
intriguing absence of Germany and Austria from Google Street View's extensive
global coverage. This gap is not due to technological limitations but stems from
a deep-rooted cultural emphasis on privacy in these countries. The article
highlights that while Germans may be open about physical nudity, as seen in
their Freikörperkultur (FKK) or "free body culture," they are fiercely
protective of their personal data. This protective stance is rooted in the
historical traumas of living under the surveillance states of the Nazi regime
and East Germany's Stasi. These experiences have ingrained a strong sense of
privacy in the German psyche, leading to stringent data protection laws and a
general wariness towards data collection and surveillance.

The article details how Germany's approach to privacy has led to unique
challenges for companies like Google. When Google announced plans to map
Germany's streets in 2010, there was significant public backlash, including
vandalism of Google's camera cars and legal challenges. This resistance led to a
high percentage of households requesting their homes be blurred on Street View,
prompting Google to limit its Street View operations in Germany. The article
also touches on the broader implications of Germany's privacy stance in a world
increasingly reliant on data monetization and digital technologies. It raises
questions about the balance between privacy and the benefits of digitization, as
well as the potential shift in attitudes towards data privacy among younger
generations.

https://www.freethink.com/society/germany-street-view

  ▀▓▀░▒▀■█▀█▒▀▄▀▓■▓▒ ░▒▀■█▀█▒▀▄▀▓■▓░▒▀■█▀█▒▀▄▀▓■▓▒░▒▀■▓▒░▓▒░▒▀■█▀█▒▀▄▀▓■▓▒▀█

The Wall Street Journal's article "Is It Safe to Share Personal Information With
a Chatbot?" delves into the privacy risks associated with using AI chatbots. It
acknowledges the inherent risk in sharing personal information, given that
chatbots are trained on vast datasets that may include sensitive data. This
training process could potentially lead to the unintentional exposure of private
information in other users' interactions or through security breaches. Experts
like Ramayya Krishnan from Carnegie Mellon University emphasize the lack of
independent audits on chatbot training data, raising concerns about the presence
of private data in these models.

The article advises users to exercise caution, highlighting instances where
chatbots have inadvertently released confidential information. While steps have
been taken by companies to protect user data, such as enabling the deletion of
chat histories, these measures are not completely secure against hacking. Users
are advised to be wary of chatbots that lack a privacy notice or ask for
excessive personal information. The key takeaway is to carefully consider the
risks before sharing sensitive information with AI chatbots and to thoroughly
review their terms of service and privacy policies.

This piece underscores the tension between the utility of AI chatbots and the
imperative to safeguard personal privacy in the digital realm.

https://www.wsj.com/tech/ai/ai-chatbot-sharing-personal-information-229d41a0

The article from BGR discusses a significant privacy concern with Google
Chrome's Incognito mode. Despite popular belief, Incognito mode doesn't offer
complete privacy. Google has recently updated its disclaimer for Incognito mode
users, explicitly stating that Google will continue tracking user activity even
in this mode. This revelation comes amidst a $5 billion lawsuit settlement
regarding Google's tracking practices in Incognito mode. The updated disclaimer
clarifies that Incognito mode only prevents others using the same device from
viewing one's browsing history, but does not stop data collection by websites,
including Google.

The author, a long-time Mac user and Microsoft Edge enthusiast, expresses
disappointment with Google Chrome, especially in light of this development. They
highlight that while private browsing is generally not completely private due to
tracking by advertising companies, Google could improve Chrome by ensuring that
data from Incognito mode is not linked to a user's broader online profile. The
article also references Google Maps' Incognito mode, which promises not to use
activity for personalizing the Maps experience, yet acknowledges that tracking
might still occur. The piece concludes with anticipation for the terms of the
recent lawsuit settlement and the hope for more meaningful changes to Chrome's
privacy features in the future.

https://bgr.com/tech/chrome-incognito-still-tracks-you-which-is-a-big-reason-i-
wont-use-googles-browser/

The article from Upworthy discusses a significant update from Google, allowing
individuals to request the removal of their personal information from Google
Search results. This change, announced on April 27, is a response to the growing
concerns about privacy and the ease with which personal details like home
addresses and phone numbers can be found online. Google's Global Policy Lead for
Search, Michelle Chang, emphasizes the company's commitment to balancing open
access to information with tools for personal privacy protection.

Users can now initiate a removal request through Google's support page,
specifying the personal information appearing in Search and providing relevant
search terms. While Google's new policy is a step towards greater control over
personal data, it's important to note that removing content from Google Search
doesn't erase it from the internet. The article also highlights the broader
context of increasing online fraud, with consumers losing $5.8 billion to
scammers in 2021. This update from Google is part of a larger effort to combat
online fraud and protect personal information in the digital age.

In the rapidly evolving world of Internet technologies, the SendingNetwork
emerges as a groundbreaking solution to the challenges of decentralized
messaging. Developed by Mason Yeung and his team at Sending Labs, this research
paper delves into the creation of a decentralized communication network that
addresses key issues such as privacy protection, scalability, efficiency, and
composability. The core of SendingNetwork's approach is the integration of edge
computing and the modular libp2p library, aiming to establish a reliable and
secure communication network.

The methodology of SendingNetwork is multifaceted. It employs a dynamic group
chat encryption mechanism based on the Double Ratchet algorithm, ensuring secure
communication. Additionally, the network introduces a Delegation scheme for
efficient message processing in large group chats, enhancing both resilience and
scalability. The paper also discusses the integration of two innovative
consensus mechanisms: "Proof of Relay" for validating message relay workload and
"Proof of Availability" for ensuring network consistency and managing
incentives.

The implications of SendingNetwork are significant for the future of

This research paper delves into the privacy implications of using ambient light
sensors in smart devices. The study focuses on how these sensors, commonly
considered low-risk, can be exploited to capture images of touch interaction on
a device's screen, a feat traditionally achieved only with cameras. This is done

risk, prompting the need for reconsideration of sensor permissions and
mitigation strategies in smart devices.

raises critical questions: Should we reevaluate the security of 'low-risk'

In a significant move, the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) has issued a stern
warning to AI companies, particularly those offering model-as-a-service, to
honor their privacy and confidentiality commitments. Model-as-a-service
companies provide AI models for personal or professional use, a service that has
become increasingly vital due to the high costs and complexities involved in AI
model development. However, the FTC is concerned about the privacy implications
of these services. The training of these AI models requires vast amounts of
data, which can lead to excessive data collection, potentially conflicting with
the companies' obligations to protect user data. This tension raises questions
about the adherence to data minimization principles, a key aspect of privacy
laws in various jurisdictions.

The FTC's warning is not just a cautionary note but comes with the threat of
legal action. Under Section 5 of the FTC Act, the Commission can bring
enforcement actions against companies that fail to keep their privacy promises.
This includes a novel remedy known as "algorithmic disgorgement," where
companies may be forced to delete algorithms developed with improperly collected
data. The implications of this enforcement extend beyond the United States.
European and African businesses and consumers engaging with these AI services
must be aware of these developments, as the misuse of data by model-as-a-service
companies can have global repercussions, including potential antitrust
violations. The FTC's stance is clear: there is no exemption for AI companies
from existing laws, and they must align their practices with their privacy
commitments and public statements.

 █   The Patchwork of State Data Privacy Laws: A Call for Federal Action    ▓
  ▀▓▀░▒▀■█▀█▒▀▄▀▓■▓▒ ░▒▀■█▀█▒▀▄▀▓■▓░▒▀■█▀█▒▀▄▀▓■▓▒░▒▀■▓▒░▓▒░▒▀■█▀█▒▀▄▀▓■▓▒▀█

In the United States, the absence of a federal data privacy law has led to a
patchwork of state-level legislation, with 13 states having enacted their own
laws. California pioneered this movement with a comprehensive law that allows
consumers to sue companies over data breaches. This approach contrasts with the
12 other states, including Colorado, Connecticut, and Virginia, which have
modeled their laws on a 2019 proposal from Washington state. These laws do not
permit individual lawsuits against tech companies for data breaches but offer a
mix of basic and substantive protections. Both California's model and the other
states' approaches allow their attorney generals to sue companies for
violations.

The diversity in state laws highlights the complexity and urgency of
establishing a federal data privacy law. Privacy advocates are pushing for a
federal approach that emphasizes data minimization, limiting the collection of
data to what is necessary for providing a service or selling a product. However,
Congress has struggled to reach a consensus on this issue. The rapid advancement
of AI systems, which rely on large volumes of data, adds pressure for federal
action. The lack of a unified federal law not only creates a fragmented legal
landscape in the U.S. but also poses challenges for international companies,
including those in Europe and Africa, who must navigate these varying state
laws. The situation underscores the need for a comprehensive federal data
privacy law that aligns with global privacy standards and addresses the
complexities of modern data usage.

 █  FTC's 'Historic' Privacy Win: A Deeper Look at the Outlogic Settlement  ▓
  ▀▓▀░▒▀■█▀█▒▀▄▀▓■▓▒ ░▒▀■█▀█▒▀▄▀▓■▓░▒▀■█▀█▒▀▄▀▓■▓▒░▒▀■▓▒░▓▒░▒▀■█▀█▒▀▄▀▓■▓▒▀█

The Federal Trade Commission's (FTC) recent settlement with Outlogic (formerly
X-Mode) over the sale of sensitive location data has been labeled historic, but
the reality paints a different picture. The settlement mandates Outlogic to stop
selling sensitive location data and delete the data it has illicitly gathered.
However, Outlogic's response, asserting that the FTC order would not
significantly impact its operations, highlights a crucial issue in the current
privacy regulation landscape. The FTC's action, while significant, primarily
targets deceptive practices rather than the broader issue of pervasive
surveillance. This approach is based on the principle of "notice and choice,"
which is often ineffective as most users do not fully read or understand privacy
policies.

The case also reveals a disparity in privacy protections. While Congress members
seek to protect themselves from surveillance, the general public remains
exposed. The FTC's limited enforcement capabilities and the challenges in
proving "concrete harm" in court leave consumers with little defense against
privacy violations. The situation is further complicated by legislative efforts
like the American Data Privacy and Protection Act of 2022, which ironically
would have exempted activities similar to Outlogic's. In contrast, the Fourth
Amendment Is Not For Sale Act, currently under consideration, aims to end the
government's practice of buying location data from companies, potentially
offering a more robust approach to privacy protection.

This scenario has broader implications, especially for regions with stringent
privacy laws like Europe and Africa. The U.S.'s struggle to enforce effective
privacy regulations underscores the global challenge of balancing technological
advancements with individual privacy rights. The Outlogic case serves as a stark
reminder of the need for more comprehensive privacy safeguards in the digital
age.
